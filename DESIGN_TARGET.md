# Design Target

## Version 0.6 Thesis

**The kingdom is the army.** Alfred's strength does not sit permanently on the
map. It is borrowed from named regions, used for a short service, and returned
before military necessity becomes political exhaustion.

This is the volume's unique selling value over *IMPERATOR*. Both games share
visible enemy intent, dual-use command cards, battle purpose, historical
mandates, and a legacy judgment. They should not share the same force economy.

## Exceptional-Game Gates

- The player can explain every field force by naming the region that bears it.
- Muster, retain, and release create the hardest decision in at least 70% of
  experienced plays.
- The three-step Service clock produces pressure without upkeep arithmetic.
- Danish Host purposes are predictable from map state and one printed card.
- Burhs transform routes, recovery, and enemy bases without a construction
  mini-game.
- A military victory can still create a strategically weak kingdom.
- Negotiation can be the strongest move without becoming a free escape.
- Each scenario teaches or stresses a different part of the same core system.
- At least three viable openings exist in S01 and S08.
- Royal Priority choice changes the opening or reserve plan in at least 60% of
  repeat plays without adding a new phase.
- Every Command and Crisis effect can be implemented literally in a guided
  edition.
- Blind testers resolve Viking movement without designer interpretation.
- The legacy result prompts discussion of what survived, not just who won.
- Blind third-play ratings reach a median of 8.5+ before any claim that the
  game can compete for a 9+ public rating.

## Deliberately Absent

- manpower points, hides, or individual levies;
- tactical hex combat;
- a naval subsystem;
- separate garrison units;
- supply-line tracing;
- a Danish Host priority flowchart;
- a generic technology tree; and
- repeated upkeep payments for every fyrd.

Depth should come from geographic obligation, service timing, enemy purpose,
and the irreversible consequences of using the same region too often.
