# Design Scope

## Design Goal

Create a tense, replayable solo wargame in which defending Wessex is a problem
of geography, time, manpower, and legitimacy. The player should feel like a
ruler assembling responses from a living kingdom, not a commander pushing a
permanent stack from battle to battle.

The game should be approachable to a first-time wargamer but interesting to a
historically curious solo player after repeated plays.

## Four Pillars

### 1. The Army Comes From the Land

The map is divided into a small number of regions. Each can be:

- **Ready**: able to provide its normal fyrd contribution;
- **Depleted**: able to provide reduced or costly support;
- **Exhausted**: unable to muster until it recovers.

Calling a regional fyrd changes its readiness. Extended service, forced
marches, battle losses, and raids may worsen it. Timely dismissal and protected
harvest periods allow recovery.

This must use markers or flipping states, never manpower arithmetic.

### 2. Danish Hosts Have Intent, Not Administration

Each Viking army should have a visible current purpose, such as:

- seek winter quarters;
- raid for wealth;
- draw out the fyrd;
- join another army;
- seize a crossing or settlement;
- bargain from strength;
- escape by river or sea.

One card and one doctrine should normally be enough to resolve an army's turn.
The player may understand the danger without knowing its exact next move.

### 3. Fortifications Change Geography

Burhs are not passive victory points. They should:

- shelter a region from the worst effects of a raid;
- delay or redirect an invading army;
- support mustering and army movement;
- secure roads, rivers, or crossings;
- require regional labor and political commitment.

Building or maintaining them must compete with field operations. A burh network
should feel like a strategic transformation of Wessex, not a technology track.

### 4. Survival Is Political

Alfred cannot defend Wessex through battle alone. The game should model:

- confidence in Alfred's kingship;
- obligations imposed on regions and leading families;
- relations with Mercian allies;
- tribute, hostages, baptism, and negotiated settlement;
- learning, law, and royal administration as sources of durable authority.

The political system should remain compact: two or three tracks at most, with
cards presenting the difficult decisions.

## Fyrd Muster Model

A provisional muster should work as follows:

1. Alfred selects a gathering region.
2. Ready adjacent regions may each contribute one force.
3. More distant regions may contribute by paying time, command, or readiness.
4. Contributing regions become Depleted.
5. The army has a limited service window.
6. Keeping it beyond that window risks Exhaustion, political loss, or desertion.
7. Dismissing it in time permits regional recovery.

This creates the central tension: the strongest response today may remove
tomorrow's response.

Elite household troops may persist between musters, but they must remain too
small to solve the war alone.

## Map Model

Use approximately 8-12 regions connected by:

- roads for West Saxon concentration;
- rivers for Viking penetration and withdrawal;
- coasts for arrival and escape;
- major crossings;
- fortified and unfortified settlements.

Movement should normally be region-to-region. Terrain effects should be
printed on the map or expressed through connections, avoiding lookup tables.

Likely geographic emphasis:

- Wessex as the defensible political heart;
- the Thames approaches and London;
- southern Mercia and allied territory;
- Kent and the Channel approaches;
- Somerset and the western refuge;
- Dorset, Hampshire, Wiltshire, and Berkshire as distinct muster burdens.

The map is an operational interpretation, not a claim of precise ninth-century
administrative boundaries.

## Campaign Rhythm

A round should represent a campaign interval rather than a fixed number of
days. A likely sequence:

1. **Omen or intelligence:** reveal Viking purpose and historical pressure.
2. **Council:** choose a limited command posture or royal priority.
3. **Muster and movement:** raise, dismiss, concentrate, or reposition forces.
4. **Viking action:** resolve each active army quickly.
5. **Confrontation:** battle, blockade, pursuit, negotiation, or withdrawal.
6. **Service and recovery:** test fyrd endurance and restore eligible regions.
7. **Kingdom:** resolve burhs, legitimacy, wealth, and scenario progress.

The final sequence should fit on one reference card.

## Battle Character

Battle must be dangerous and purposeful. Before rolling, Alfred chooses an aim:

- **Delay:** reduce Viking movement or prevent a raid;
- **Drive Off:** force retreat without requiring destruction;
- **Decisive Battle:** accept greater losses for a major strategic result;
- **Blockade:** contain a base until supply or negotiation changes the position.

Force, position, readiness, leadership, and preparation should matter more than
small tactical modifiers. One or two rolls should resolve most confrontations.

## Decision Cards

A shared hand mechanism may connect the series, but Alfred's cards must express
his own dilemmas. A card might be used for:

- a military command;
- a civil reform or political concession;
- an enduring royal project;
- preserving it for a later historical crisis.

Possible subjects include road maintenance, fortified bridges, household
troops, regional assemblies, hostages, tribute, Mercian cooperation, ship
building, law, education, and succession.

## Victory and Legacy

Scenarios should combine an immediate mandate with a judgment of the kingdom
left behind.

Potential final measures:

- Wessex survives as an independent kingdom;
- key regions remain capable of mustering;
- the burh network is credible;
- Viking armies lack secure permanent bases in Wessex;
- Alfred retains legitimacy;
- alliances and succession remain viable.

A desperate survival can be a victory. A battlefield triumph that leaves every
region exhausted should not be the highest result.

## Replayability

Replayability should come from:

- different Viking army doctrines and entry routes;
- variable regional readiness at setup;
- competing royal priorities;
- scenario-specific historical mandates;
- command cards used in mutually exclusive ways;
- uncertain alliances and negotiated outcomes;
- several valid strategic postures rather than one optimal opening.

Randomness should vary the problem, not erase the consequences of decisions.

## Explicit Exclusions

The design should avoid:

- counting individual hides, men, supplies, or silver;
- moving many small Viking units;
- tactical hex combat;
- a detailed naval subsystem;
- a burh construction mini-game;
- exact simulation of disputed administrative boundaries;
- an opposition flowchart requiring constant cross-reference;
- merely replacing *IMPERATOR* terminology and artwork.

## Development Gates

The concept should not progress to final art until tests show:

1. regional mustering creates hard geographic choices;
2. Viking turns resolve quickly and produce legible threats;
3. burhs alter strategy without becoming mandatory build orders;
4. scenarios reward meaningfully different approaches;
5. experienced players cannot identify one dominant muster pattern;
6. the game reliably finishes within the intended play time;
7. blind testers can run the opposition without designer assistance.
