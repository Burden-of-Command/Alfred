# Scenario Outline

The target is six or seven focused scenarios plus one linked reign campaign.
Each scenario must change the strategic question, not merely the setup.

## 1. The Year of Battles, 871

**Question:** How long can Wessex keep responding when every muster consumes
the strength needed for the next engagement?

Alfred begins as a battlefield leader during Æthelred's final campaign and may
inherit a kingdom already depleted. Frequent engagements, uncertain command,
and short service limits dominate.

Distinct pressure: rapid remustering with almost no recovery.

## 2. A King at the Brink, 875-878

**Question:** Which parts of the kingdom can be abandoned temporarily so that
effective resistance can survive?

Viking maneuver, winter occupation, political collapse, and a western refuge
lead toward a possible concentration and decisive return.

Distinct pressure: preserve legitimacy and a viable muster base while losing
geographic control.

## 3. Edington, 878

**Question:** Can Alfred assemble one army strong enough to force a settlement
without exhausting the kingdom beyond recovery?

A short, tense scenario centered on concentration, timing, battle purpose, and
the terms after victory.

Distinct pressure: a single operational culmination with multiple political
outcomes.

## 4. The Shield of Wessex, c. 879-886

**Question:** How much immediate security can be sacrificed to create a
defensive system for the next invasion?

The player develops burhs, routes, regional readiness, and alliances while
facing raids and political demands.

Distinct pressure: construction and reform rather than constant major battle.

## 5. London and the Mercians, c. 880s

**Question:** Can Alfred extend influence beyond Wessex without overextending
its military obligations?

London, Mercian cooperation, legitimacy, and frontier security pull forces in
different directions.

Distinct pressure: allied geography and political consolidation.

## 6. The Return of the Armies, 892-894

**Question:** How can several Viking armies be contained when defeating one
may open the road for another?

Separate Viking forces establish bases, threaten to combine, and exploit
rivers and undefended regions.

Distinct pressure: multi-army sequencing and interior movement.

## 7. The Long Pursuit, 894-896

**Question:** When should Alfred pursue, blockade, negotiate, or simply deny
the enemy a profitable position?

The conflict spreads beyond a single decisive battlefield. Fyrd service,
fortified camps, Mercian support, and regional exhaustion become central.

Distinct pressure: endurance and denial rather than annihilation.

## 8. The Burden of Wessex, 871-899

**Question:** What kind of kingdom survives Alfred's reign?

A linked campaign joins crisis, recovery, reform, renewed invasion, and
succession. Persistent regional condition and selected royal projects carry
between chapters, but bookkeeping must remain minimal.

Distinct pressure: immediate survival versus the institutions and relationships
needed for durable rule.

## Scenario Standard

Every scenario should specify:

- historical interval and acknowledged compression;
- regional readiness and burh state;
- Viking armies, doctrines, and intentions;
- Alfred's available royal priorities;
- one unique rule no longer than a short paragraph;
- an immediate Historical Mandate;
- a legacy result based on both survival and the condition of the kingdom.
