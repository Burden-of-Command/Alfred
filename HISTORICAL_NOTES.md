# Historical Notes

## Scope

The game covers Alfred's reign, 871-899, from repeated campaigning against the
Great Army through the crisis of 878, the settlement associated with Guthrum,
the recovery of London, defensive reform, and the renewed invasions of the
890s.

## The Fyrd

The game does not claim that every named region supplied one identical unit or
followed one universal service rule. Regional Readiness compresses military
obligation, travel, provisioning, harvest, local defense, leadership, and the
political cost of repeated summons. The design models a temporary field force,
not a standing national army.

## Burhs and Roads

Fortified places and maintained routes formed a connected defense. The
*Burghal Hidage* is important evidence, but its date, development, and direct
relationship to Alfred remain debated. The six Burh sites on the game map are
operational abstractions. An active Burh represents fortification, labor,
garrison obligation, and reliable access rather than one construction event.

## Danish Hosts

The Chronicle usually speaks of an army or host rather than preserving stable
formal names for every force. Danish forces raided, wintered, occupied fortified positions, used rivers and
coasts, combined and divided, negotiated, accepted baptism, settled, and
withdrew. Guthrum's Host and Hæsten's Host use known leaders. The Seaborne Host
and Thames Host are transparent game labels for recurring strategic profiles,
not attested army names or claims that four unchanged organizations operated
throughout the reign.

## Negotiation

Tribute, oaths, hostages, baptism, boundaries, and settlement were instruments
of war and rule. The game treats terms as potentially prudent and potentially
temporary. It avoids presenting conversion as a mechanical guarantee of good
faith.

## Map and Chronology

The ten-place map is an operational interpretation, not ninth-century
administrative cartography. It uses attested or archaeologically important
Alfredian places such as Wareham, Winchester, Wilton, Reading, Wallingford,
and Exeter instead of later county labels. Each place represents a wider
muster region; the map does not claim precise ninth-century boundaries.
Athelney represents the refuge called Æthelingaeg in the Chronicle.
`Ethandun (Edington)` follows the conventional identification, but the precise
site remains disputed. Scenario dates and routes are compressed where the
sources do not support a precise sequence.

The map uses **London** as a player-facing umbrella term across the whole
reign. Contemporary usage distinguishes the trading settlement commonly
called **Lundenwic** from the refortified Roman enclosure associated with
Alfred's occupation in 886, commonly called **Lundenburh**. Scenario text about
restoring or fortifying London represents that change rather than claiming
that the same settlement name and form applied unchanged from 871 to 899.

## Starting References

- *The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle*, especially entries for 871-878 and 892-896.
- Asser, *Life of King Alfred*.
- The *Burghal Hidage* and scholarship on its date and interpretation.
- The treaty text associated with Alfred and Guthrum.
- Richard Abels, *Alfred the Great: War, Kingship and Culture in Anglo-Saxon
  England*.
- Ryan Lavelle, *Alfred's Wars*.
- Gareth Williams, ed., *A Riverine Site Near York: A Possible Viking Camp?*
  and broader archaeological work on Viking campaigning and fortified sites.

The references are a research beginning, not a claim that disputed questions
have been settled.
